# How the federal HUD Code evolved from 1990 to 2015

**The HUD Manufactured Home Construction and Safety Standards underwent roughly a dozen major regulatory actions between 1990 and 2015**, transforming manufactured housing from a code regime largely frozen since 1976 into a modernized, consensus-driven system. The most consequential changes were the 1994 post–Hurricane Andrew wind standards overhaul, the 1993 energy conservation upgrade, and the 2000 Manufactured Housing Improvement Act that created the consensus committee process governing all subsequent updates. Together, these actions reshaped structural, fire safety, energy, plumbing, electrical, and installation requirements across the entire 24 CFR Part 3280 framework—though GAO repeatedly found that HUD updated standards too slowly, leaving gaps that persisted for years.

---

## Hurricane Andrew forced the biggest structural overhaul in HUD Code history

The single most impactful regulatory change in this period was the **1994 wind standards final rule** (59 FR 2469, January 14, 1994), prompted by the catastrophic destruction Hurricane Andrew inflicted in August 1992. In Dade County, Florida, **97% of manufactured homes were totally destroyed** compared to 11% of site-built homes—a disparity that made immediate code reform politically inevitable.

HUD replaced its outdated two-tier wind classification with a **three-zone system** anchored to the ANSI/ASCE 7-88 consensus standard for wind loads, the first time the HUD Code adopted a mainstream engineering standard for this purpose. Wind Zone I covered most inland areas at 70 mph design speeds. Wind Zone II covered hurricane-susceptible coastal regions at **100 mph**, and the new Wind Zone III targeted the most vulnerable Gulf and Atlantic coastlines at **110 mph**—matching site-built home requirements for the first time. A new Basic Wind Zone Map designated specific counties for Zones II and III.

The structural requirements were overhauled accordingly. Steel strapping or engineered connectors at roof-to-wall and wall-to-floor joints became mandatory at **24-inch spacing in Zone II and 16-inch spacing in Zone III**. Exterior cladding fastening was upgraded to resist higher wind pressures, and eave/cornice provisions required design for net uplift pressures 2.5 times the Zone I baseline. Windows and sliding glass doors in Zones II and III gained new debris-impact testing requirements (effective January 1995). Data plates were expanded to show wind zone designation, roof load zone, and reproductions of load zone maps. The rule took effect July 13, 1994, and proved strikingly successful: post-hurricane assessments after the 2004 Florida hurricane season found **no post-1994 manufactured homes suffered serious structural damage**.

The Florida Manufactured Housing Association challenged the rule in *FMHA v. Cisneros*, but the Eleventh Circuit upheld HUD's rulemaking authority.

## Energy conservation standards were rewritten—then frozen for two decades

The second major early-1990s overhaul addressed energy performance. Spurred partly by the **Energy Policy Act of 1992** (which threatened to impose the CABO Model Energy Code if HUD failed to act), HUD published a comprehensive energy conservation final rule on October 25, 1993 (58 FR 54975), effective October 25, 1994.

This rule created the **three Uo Value thermal zones** that would govern manufactured home energy performance for the next two decades. Zone 1 (southern states) required a maximum overall heat transmission coefficient (Uo) of **0.116 Btu/(hr)(sq ft)(°F)**; Zone 2 (mid-latitude states) required **0.096**; and Zone 3 (northern states) required **0.079**. The Uo-based approach was performance-oriented, allowing manufacturers to trade off wall, ceiling, floor, and fenestration insulation levels so long as the overall envelope met the standard. Zone 3 homes required factory-installed storm windows or insulating glass. New whole-house ventilation requirements mandated **0.035 cfm per square foot** of floor area, with mechanical ventilation systems and zone-specific provisions. Kitchen and bathroom exhaust fan requirements were formalized at 50–100 cfm.

The rule also established heat loss calculation procedures based on Pacific Northwest National Laboratory research, an alternative annual-energy-use compliance pathway, high-efficiency equipment credits, heating certificates, and condensation control provisions for vapor retarders and attic ventilation.

These 1994 Uo values were **never substantively updated during the 1990–2015 period**. The 2005 first MHCC rulemaking made procedural and reference-standard tweaks (adding NFRC 100 for fenestration testing, updating the ASHRAE Handbook edition) but left the core thermal performance requirements untouched. The **Energy Independence and Security Act of 2007** transferred authority to set manufactured housing energy standards from HUD to the Department of Energy, which began rulemaking in 2010 but did not publish a final rule until 2022. During the interim, the 1994 standards—which lacked any solar heat gain coefficient requirement, blower door testing, or alignment with the IECC—remained the operative federal energy code for manufactured homes.

## The 2000 Improvement Act reshaped how the HUD Code gets updated

The **Manufactured Housing Improvement Act of 2000** (P.L. 106-569, Title VI, signed December 27, 2000) was the most significant legislative change to manufactured housing regulation since the original 1974 Act. It fundamentally restructured the code-development process and created three new regulatory programs.

The Act established the **Manufactured Housing Consensus Committee (MHCC)**, a 21-member federal advisory committee balanced among producers/retailers (7 members), users/consumers (7), and general interest/public officials (7), with one non-voting HUD representative. The MHCC became the central mechanism for proposing HUD Code revisions. It held its first meeting in August 2002 and organized recommendations into multi-year "sets" submitted to HUD for rulemaking. The Act required standards to be **performance-based and objectively stated**, and directed HUD to act on MHCC recommendations within one year when submitted with supporting economic analysis—a deadline HUD never actually met, according to a 2014 GAO report.

Three new program mandates flowed from the Act:

- **Federal installation standards** (Section 605) required HUD to establish minimum manufactured home installation standards. This produced 24 CFR Part 3285 (Model Installation Standards, finalized October 2007, effective October 2008) covering site preparation, foundations, piers, anchoring, structural interconnection of multi-section homes, and utility connections. States could maintain their own programs if they met or exceeded the federal model. A companion Installation Program rule at 24 CFR Part 3286 (June 2008) established installer licensing, inspection requirements, and a tracking system.

- **Dispute resolution** (Section 623) required a program for resolving defect disputes among manufacturers, retailers, and installers within **120 days** of a request. HUD codified this at 24 CFR Part 3288 (May 2007), covering mediation, arbitration, and requirements for state-administered alternatives. Defects had to be reported within one year of installation.

- **Enhanced federal preemption** (Section 604) directed that preemption of state and local construction requirements be "broadly and liberally construed." In a notable 2014 application, HUD preempted Minnesota's attempt to require blower-door testing for manufactured homes under the IECC, holding the state standard impermissibly exceeded federal requirements.

## Two rounds of MHCC recommendations drove the 2005 and 2013 updates

The consensus committee process produced two major rulemaking packages within the 1990–2015 window, along with several smaller targeted rules.

**The first MHCC set** became a final rule on November 30, 2005 (70 FR 72024), effective May 30, 2006. It was the broadest single update since the 1994 wind rule, touching fire safety, structural design, thermal protection, electrical systems, and dozens of reference standards. Key provisions included updated fireblocking requirements, revised roof load and snow load provisions, new requirements for manufacturers to identify pier support locations, and updated condensation control and vapor retarder installation standards. The electrical systems sections adopted **NFPA 70-2005 (National Electrical Code)**, bringing arc-fault circuit interrupter protection, updated service equipment requirements, the prohibition on distribution panelboards in bathrooms or clothes closets, and the two-circuit minimum for kitchen countertop outlets. Fire safety reference standards were updated to current UL 217 and UL 268 editions. HUD retained the 1984 formaldehyde health notice requirement over MHCC objections.

**The second MHCC set** was finalized on December 9, 2013 (78 FR 73966), effective June 9, 2014—an **eight-year gap** from the previous update that drew sharp criticism from GAO. This rule focused on plumbing, HVAC, electrical, and testing standards. Plumbing provisions added **PEX (crosslinked polyethylene) tubing** as an approved material, required anti-scald valves conforming to ASSE 1016-2005 for showers and tub-showers, and set **2.5 gpm maximum flow rates** for faucets and showerheads. HVAC sections authorized corrugated stainless steel tubing for gas piping and updated gas furnace standards. Approximately **30 reference standards** were updated across all subparts, including the fenestration standard (AAMA/WDMA/CSA 101/I.S.2/A440-08), safety glazing (ANSI Z97.1-2004), and steel strapping (ASTM D3953-1997). HUD explicitly excluded energy-related MHCC recommendations from this package because DOE rulemaking was pending under EISA.

## Fire safety and smoke alarms saw targeted but meaningful reforms

Beyond the omnibus MHCC packages, HUD issued a standalone **smoke alarm overhaul** on March 19, 2002 (67 FR 12817). The rule responded to data showing **35–40% of manufactured home fires involved non-operational smoke detectors**, largely because occupants disabled them due to cooking-triggered false alarms.

The revised standard required smoke alarms in **each room designed for sleeping** plus the living area—a significant expansion from the prior requirement of hallway-only placement. Alarms within 20 feet of a cooking appliance were required to have a **temporary silencing ("hush") feature or use photoelectric sensing**, directly targeting the nuisance-alarm problem. All alarms had to be **interconnected** so activation of one triggered all units, and **battery backup** was required for hardwired alarms. Manufacturers were required to operationally test each factory-installed alarm and provide homeowner maintenance instructions. The rule also addressed visible/tactile notification appliances for hearing-impaired occupants when ordered.

One notable absence: **carbon monoxide detectors were not required** during the 1990–2015 period. The MHCC recommended CO alarm requirements around 2010, but the final rule incorporating them (86 FR 2496) did not take effect until July 2021. Similarly, the 1984 formaldehyde emission limits (0.2 ppm for plywood, 0.3 ppm for particleboard) remained unchanged throughout this period despite the Formaldehyde Standards for Composite Wood Products Act of 2010; HUD deferred to pending EPA rulemaking and did not finalize updated formaldehyde standards until 2020.

## Structural testing and on-site completion rules rounded out the period

Two additional targeted rules addressed specific technical and procedural gaps. A **roof truss testing final rule** (78 FR 4060, January 18, 2013, effective January 2014) overhauled qualification and quality-control testing for roof trusses. The revisions eliminated nondestructive testing as an initial qualification method and established differentiated uplift testing requirements by wind zone: Wind Zone I required proof loads of 1.75 times design load sustained for 12 hours or ultimate loads of 2.5 times for 5 minutes, while Zones II and III required **2.0 times design load for 1 minute in inverted orientation**.

The **on-site completion of construction rule** (80 FR 53727, September 8, 2015, effective March 2016) formalized procedures for homes substantially completed in the factory with remaining work finished at the installation site. Previously, manufacturers needed individual Alternative Construction letters from HUD for such arrangements. The new Subpart M of 24 CFR Part 3282 allowed factory-certified homes to have site-installed features like dormers, brick siding, stucco, tile roofs, and homeowner-ordered appliances, provided all construction that could be completed in the plant was finished there. Inspection of on-site work by the production inspection agency was required before occupancy.

A **Subpart I procedural revision** (78 FR 60199, October 2013, effective March 2014) also streamlined the notification, investigation, and correction procedures for manufacturer handling of consumer complaints and defect reports—establishing clearer roles for manufacturers, state agencies, and production inspection agencies.

## Conclusion

The 1990–2015 period saw the HUD Code transform from a largely static 1970s-era regulation into a more dynamic, consensus-driven system—though one that still moved slowly. The two watershed moments were the **1994 post–Hurricane Andrew wind overhaul**, which brought manufactured home structural standards to parity with site-built codes for the first time and demonstrably saved homes in subsequent hurricanes, and the **2000 Manufactured Housing Improvement Act**, which created the institutional infrastructure for ongoing code evolution. The energy standards, by contrast, represent a cautionary tale: the 1994 thermal requirements went unchanged for the entire period—and beyond—as jurisdictional transfer from HUD to DOE created a regulatory gap that lasted over a decade. The MHCC consensus process produced genuine technical improvements in fire safety, plumbing, electrical, and structural testing, but the eight-year gap between the 2005 and 2013 updates illustrated the chronic resource and prioritization challenges that plagued HUD's manufactured housing program throughout this era.

## Subjective cost impact ranking

**The 1994 wind standards were almost certainly the largest cost driver overall.** They required physical changes to every home built for Zones II and III — steel strapping at tighter spacing, upgraded wall and roof sheathing fastening, impact-rated windows, and new engineering documentation. The Florida industry fought the rule in court partly on cost grounds. Because the Gulf and Atlantic coastline markets are among the highest-volume in the industry, the Zone II/III upgrades affected a very large share of total production. The engineering and testing burden also fell on all manufacturers, not just coastal ones.

**The 2005 NEC adoption (NFPA 70-2005) was likely the second-largest.** Arc-fault circuit interrupter (AFCI) breakers are meaningfully more expensive than standard breakers, and the two-circuit minimum for kitchen countertops added wiring labor. Electrical is already one of the more labor-intensive factory operations, so any NEC update ripples through floor plans, panel sizing, and inspection protocols industry-wide.

**The 2002 smoke alarm rule had a notable per-unit cost impact** because it required interconnected, hardwired alarms with battery backup in multiple rooms rather than a single hallway unit. Interconnection wiring is added factory labor, and the hush-feature or photoelectric requirement meant higher-cost alarm units across the board.

**The 1993 energy rule was cost-significant but somewhat offset.** It raised insulation requirements substantially in Zones 2 and 3, adding material cost. However, the Uo-based performance approach gave manufacturers flexibility to find the cheapest combination of insulation upgrades, which moderated the impact somewhat compared to a prescriptive rule.

The **2013 MHCC package** (PEX tubing, anti-scald valves, flow restrictors) had real but smaller per-unit costs — PEX actually tends to be cheaper and faster to install than copper, so the plumbing changes may have been closer to cost-neutral.

The **installation standards and on-site completion rules** shifted costs but didn't raise them uniformly — they affected installers and retailers more than factory production.

So the rough ranking by production cost impact would be: **(1) 1994 wind standards, (2) 2005 NEC/AFCI adoption, (3) 2002 smoke alarm interconnection rule, (4) 1993 energy conservation rule**. The wind rule stands apart because it required structural redesign — not just upgraded components — for a large portion of total industry output.
