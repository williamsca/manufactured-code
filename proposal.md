# Reframing: Mandated Adaptation and Insurance Market Access

## Core Argument

The standard result (Ehrlich & Becker 1972) is that self-protection and market insurance are substitutes. The HUD code reform reverses this: post-1994 MH shows both lower damage and higher NFIP take-up. The mechanism is that NFIP eligibility requirements (anchoring, elevation, community participation) are costly to meet, and the HUD code lowers that compliance cost by building resilience into the home at the factory. When insurance markets have entry barriers, mandated construction quality and insurance are complements, not substitutes.

## Comparative Advantage vs. Baylis & Boomhower

They have better data and a more policy-relevant setting for the direct resilience channel. We cannot compete there. Our advantage is the insurance margin: chattel loan exemption from mandatory purchase means the MH insurance decision is voluntary, so we can observe take-up responses. The NFIP setting gives us both the intensive margin (damage | claim) and the extensive margin (insurance participation).
